# Entrepreneurial Intention in University Students: A Comprehensive Literature Mapping Report

## Executive Summary

This literature mapping report synthesizes scholarly research on entrepreneurial intention (EI) among university students, with particular emphasis on theoretical evolution, Latin American and Colombian contexts, and the relationship between public entrepreneurship policy and EI. Drawing from 432 peer-reviewed publications spanning 2010–2026, this report traces the development of EI research from foundational theories—Ajzen's Theory of Planned Behavior (1991) and Shapero & Sokol's Entrepreneurial Event Model (1982)—to contemporary integrated models that incorporate sustainability, digital entrepreneurship, and pandemic-related factors. The analysis reveals that while personal attitudes and perceived behavioral control consistently predict EI across contexts, the role of subjective norms and public policy support remains inconsistent, particularly in Latin American settings. Colombian studies (N=540 university students, Dataset ALBA 2025, Zenodo DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.17702439) demonstrate that family support, university environment, and socioeconomic factors significantly influence EI, yet critical research gaps persist regarding the mechanisms through which public entrepreneurship policies translate into entrepreneurial intentions and behaviors. This report provides evidence-based recommendations for researchers, policymakers, and higher education institutions seeking to foster entrepreneurial ecosystems in emerging economies.

## 1. Introduction

Entrepreneurial intention (EI) represents a critical precursor to entrepreneurial behavior and new venture creation, particularly among university students who constitute a vital source of innovation and economic dynamism in emerging economies. Understanding the factors that shape EI has become increasingly important for policymakers, educators, and researchers seeking to foster entrepreneurial ecosystems and address youth unemployment challenges. This literature mapping report provides a comprehensive synthesis of EI research spanning 2010–2026, with particular attention to theoretical evolution, regional contexts in Latin America and Colombia, and the under-explored relationship between public entrepreneurship policy and student entrepreneurial intentions.

The report is grounded in a systematic analysis of 432 peer-reviewed publications organized into four thematic clusters: (1) theoretical foundations and evolution of EI models (76 papers), (2) EI research in Latin America and Colombia (125 papers), (3) public entrepreneurship policy and research gaps (157 papers), and (4) contemporary EI models among university students (74 papers). This evidence base enables a rigorous examination of how EI research has evolved from foundational cognitive theories to contemporary integrated frameworks that address sustainability, digital transformation, and global disruptions such as the COVID-19 pandemic.

The Colombian context receives special emphasis, given the availability of recent empirical data (Dataset ALBA 2025, N=540 university students, Zenodo DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.17702439) and the country's strategic importance as an emerging economy with high entrepreneurial activity rates. Colombia ranks among the top Latin American countries in entrepreneurial intention, yet faces persistent challenges in translating intentions into sustainable business creation [11], [16]. Understanding the determinants of EI in this context—and particularly the role of public policy—has significant implications for economic development and social mobility.

This report addresses four primary research objectives: (1) trace the theoretical evolution from Ajzen's Theory of Planned Behavior (1991) and Shapero & Sokol's Entrepreneurial Event Model (1982) to contemporary integrated models; (2) identify key authors and their seminal contributions to EI research; (3) synthesize findings from Latin American and Colombian studies conducted between 2010 and 2026; and (4) critically examine research gaps regarding the relationship between public entrepreneurship policy and EI, with implications for future research and policy design.

## 2. Theoretical Foundations and Evolution

### 2.1 Foundational Theories: TPB and EEM

The study of entrepreneurial intention is fundamentally rooted in two seminal cognitive theories developed in the early 1980s and 1990s: Ajzen's Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB, 1991) and Shapero & Sokol's Entrepreneurial Event Model (EEM, 1982). These frameworks have provided the theoretical scaffolding for decades of empirical research on entrepreneurial cognition and decision-making.

**Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB).** Ajzen's TPB posits that behavioral intentions are determined by three core constructs: (1) attitude toward the behavior (personal evaluation of performing the behavior), (2) subjective norms (perceived social pressure to perform or not perform the behavior), and (3) perceived behavioral control (perceived ease or difficulty of performing the behavior, closely related to self-efficacy). The theory has demonstrated robust predictive validity across diverse cultural contexts and populations [22], [30]. In entrepreneurship research, TPB has been extensively applied to explain why individuals form intentions to start businesses, with meta-analytic evidence supporting its cross-cultural generalizability [30].

Empirical studies consistently demonstrate that attitudes and perceived behavioral control are culture-universal predictors of entrepreneurial intentions, with attitudes emerging as the strongest predictor (standardized parameter β = .40, p < .001) followed by entrepreneurial self-efficacy (β = .17, p < .001) [30]. However, subjective norms show weaker and more variable effects across cultures (β = .15, p < .05), suggesting that social influence operates differently depending on individualism-collectivism dimensions and cultural context [30].

**Entrepreneurial Event Model (EEM).** Shapero & Sokol's EEM offers an alternative cognitive framework, proposing that entrepreneurial intentions arise from three factors: (1) perceived desirability (the degree to which one finds starting a business attractive), (2) perceived feasibility (the degree to which one feels capable of starting a business), and (3) propensity to act (the disposition to act on opportunities). The model emphasizes the role of "displacement events"—positive or negative life changes that precipitate entrepreneurial action [19], [21].

Comparative empirical analyses suggest that while both models demonstrate explanatory power, they emphasize different aspects of the intention formation process. Meeks found that Ajzen's TPB was more powerful in explaining entrepreneurial intentions (R² = 0.34), while Shapero's model better predicted entrepreneurial activity [8]. A comparative study concluded that the EEM was slightly superior to TPB in predicting entrepreneurial behavior, though both models showed substantial overlap in their core constructs [21].

**Conceptual Convergence.** Krueger and colleagues demonstrated that the two models are conceptually compatible, with TPB's attitudes and subjective norms mapping onto EEM's perceived desirability, and perceived behavioral control corresponding to perceived feasibility [4], [19]. This recognition of theoretical convergence has motivated subsequent efforts to integrate the models into unified frameworks that leverage the strengths of both approaches [12].

### 2.2 Key Authors and Seminal Contributions

Several scholars have made foundational contributions to EI research, with their work cited extensively across the literature examined in this review.

**Norris F. Krueger Jr.** Krueger's work has been instrumental in bridging TPB and EEM, demonstrating their conceptual compatibility and developing integrated models of entrepreneurial potential [19]. His 1994 paper "Entrepreneurial Potential and Potential Entrepreneurs" proposed that entrepreneurial potential—determined by perceived venture desirability, perceived venture feasibility, and propensity to act—coupled with precipitating events, creates entrepreneurial intention [19]. Krueger's model has been applied extensively in diverse contexts, including Morocco [4] and Malaysia [3], validating its cross-cultural applicability.

**Francisco Liñán and Yi-Wen Chen.** Liñán and Chen's 2009 work on testing the entrepreneurial intention model in cross-cultural contexts has become a cornerstone of EI research. Their development and validation of the Entrepreneurial Intention Questionnaire (EIQ) provided researchers with a reliable instrument for measuring EI and its antecedents [11], [17]. The EIQ has been adapted and validated in numerous countries, including Colombia, where it demonstrated high reliability and predictive validity [11]. Liñán's subsequent collaborations have extended TPB by incorporating cultural, motivational, and environmental factors [17].

**Alain Fayolle and Francisco Liñán.** Fayolle and Liñán's 2014 review of entrepreneurial intentions research synthesized decades of empirical findings and identified critical gaps in the literature, particularly regarding the intention-behavior gap and the role of contextual factors. Their work has guided subsequent research toward more nuanced models that account for educational interventions, institutional support, and temporal dynamics [17].

**Icek Ajzen.** While Ajzen's TPB was not developed specifically for entrepreneurship, his theoretical framework has been the most widely applied model in EI research. Studies consistently demonstrate the robustness of TPB in predicting entrepreneurial intentions and subsequent behavior, with longitudinal evidence from Austria and Finland confirming its validity [22]. Ajzen's model has been extended in numerous ways, including the addition of entrepreneurial education [6], university support [7], and policy perception [15] as additional predictors.

### 2.3 Theoretical Integration and Extensions

The evolution of EI research has been characterized by progressive integration and extension of foundational theories to address their limitations and incorporate emerging factors.

**Integrated Models.** Recognizing the conceptual overlap between TPB and EEM, researchers have developed integrated models that combine constructs from both frameworks. Iakovleva and colleagues successfully integrated TPB and EEM, demonstrating that attitude, subjective norm, and perceived behavioral control determine desirability-feasibility, which in turn determines entrepreneurial intentions [12]. Sharahiley extended this integration by adding innovativeness and business environment variables, finding that the integrated model outperformed individual TPB and EEM models, with innovativeness emerging as the strongest predictor of entrepreneurial behavior [9].

**Extensions to Address the Intention-Behavior Gap.** A persistent challenge in EI research is the gap between intention and actual entrepreneurial behavior. LiHua addressed this by extending TPB to include entrepreneurial situational factors and entrepreneurial implementation intention, providing a more complete model of the intention-to-behavior translation process [2]. This extension acknowledges that intentions alone are insufficient; contextual factors and implementation planning play critical mediating roles [27].

**Incorporation of Personality and Cognitive Factors.** Contemporary models increasingly integrate personality traits and cognitive factors beyond the core TPB/EEM constructs. Studies have incorporated individual entrepreneurial orientation (proactive personality and risk-taking propensity) [3], need for achievement and locus of control [17], emotional competence [11], and proactive personality [3] as antecedents to traditional TPB variables. These extensions recognize that entrepreneurial intention formation is influenced by stable individual differences that shape how individuals perceive opportunities and evaluate their capabilities.

**Educational and Institutional Extensions.** Recognizing the critical role of higher education in fostering entrepreneurship, researchers have extended classic models to include entrepreneurship education [6], [16], perceived university support [5], [7], and entrepreneurial ecosystem factors [4]. These extensions acknowledge that EI is not solely an individual cognitive process but is shaped by institutional contexts and educational interventions that can be designed and optimized.

**Contemporary Factors: Sustainability, Digital Transformation, and Crisis Effects.** The most recent wave of EI research (2020–2026) has extended classic models to incorporate emerging global challenges and opportunities. Studies now examine sustainable entrepreneurial intentions [10], [22], digital entrepreneurship [4], and the effects of the COVID-19 pandemic on EI [1], [20]. These extensions reflect the evolving nature of entrepreneurship itself and the need for theoretical models to remain relevant to contemporary economic and social realities.

## 3. Methodological Approaches in EI Research

EI research has predominantly employed quantitative methodologies, with structural equation modeling (SEM) emerging as the dominant analytical approach. This methodological preference reflects the field's focus on testing theoretical models with multiple latent constructs and complex causal pathways.

**Structural Equation Modeling (SEM).** The majority of studies reviewed utilize SEM techniques, including covariance-based SEM (CB-SEM) and partial least squares SEM (PLS-SEM), to test hypothesized relationships among EI determinants [1], [8], [16], [17]. PLS-SEM has gained particular popularity due to its flexibility with smaller sample sizes and non-normal distributions [8], [21]. Studies typically employ multi-item scales adapted from validated instruments, including Liñán and Chen's EIQ [11], [17] and Ajzen's TPB scales [22], [30].

**Cross-Sectional Designs.** The vast majority of EI studies employ cross-sectional designs, collecting data at a single time point. While this approach is efficient and cost-effective, it limits causal inference and cannot capture the dynamic evolution of intentions over time. Notable exceptions include Kautonen and colleagues' longitudinal study tracking Austrian and Finnish respondents over time, which provided stronger evidence for the TPB's ability to predict both intentions and subsequent entrepreneurial behavior [22].

**Sample Characteristics.** Studies typically focus on university students, with sample sizes ranging from fewer than 100 to over 12,000 participants [16]. Business and management students are overrepresented, though some studies include engineering [28], agricultural [16], and interdisciplinary samples [22]. Geographic diversity has increased over time, with studies conducted across six continents, though developed Western economies remain overrepresented relative to emerging markets.

**Measurement Instruments.** Researchers rely heavily on validated multi-item scales measured on Likert-type response formats (typically 5- or 7-point scales). The Entrepreneurial Intention Questionnaire (EIQ) developed by Liñán and Chen has become a de facto standard, with validated versions available in multiple languages [11], [17]. However, concerns persist about measurement equivalence across cultures and the potential for social desirability bias in self-reported intentions.

**Emerging Methodological Innovations.** Recent studies have begun to incorporate methodological innovations, including: (1) mixed-methods designs combining qualitative and quantitative data [12], (2) Gaussian graphical models (GGMs) to examine network structures among variables [1], (3) longitudinal designs to address the intention-behavior gap [22], and (4) digital applications for real-time data collection [4]. These innovations promise to address some of the limitations of traditional cross-sectional survey research.

## 4. Determinants of Entrepreneurial Intention: A Comparative Analysis

### 4.1 Individual-Level Determinants

**Attitudes Toward Entrepreneurship.** Personal attitudes consistently emerge as the strongest predictor of entrepreneurial intention across diverse contexts. A cross-cultural study spanning six countries found attitudes had the highest standardized parameter (β = .40, p < .001) on intentions [30]. In Colombian samples, personal attitude showed strong positive effects (β = .755, p = 0.000) [1], [17]. Attitudes reflect individuals' overall evaluation of entrepreneurship as a career path, encompassing perceptions of attractiveness, desirability, and personal fit.

**Perceived Behavioral Control and Self-Efficacy.** Perceived behavioral control (PBC)—the belief in one's capability to successfully execute entrepreneurial tasks—is the second most consistent predictor of EI. Studies report significant positive effects of PBC on EI (β = .226, p = 0.001) [1], with entrepreneurial self-efficacy showing similar patterns [11]. However, some studies find that PBC does not significantly impact EI when other factors are controlled [7], suggesting potential mediation or moderation effects that warrant further investigation.

**Personality Traits.** Individual differences in personality significantly shape entrepreneurial intentions. Need for achievement, internal locus of control, and risk propensity consistently predict higher EI [15], [17]. Proactive personality—the tendency to take initiative and persevere—directly explains EI and is mediated by PBC and subjective norms [3]. Emotional competence, including the ability to regulate emotions and maintain low rumination, contributes to entrepreneurial intention [4], [11]. These findings underscore that entrepreneurship attracts and is facilitated by individuals with specific psychological profiles.

**Innovativeness.** Personal innovativeness—the tendency to seek novel solutions and embrace change—has emerged as a particularly strong predictor in recent studies. Sharahiley found innovativeness to be the strongest predictor of entrepreneurial behavior in an integrated TPB-EEM model [9]. Innovativeness moderates the attitude-intention relationship and directly promotes positive attitudes toward entrepreneurship [5], highlighting its dual role as both a direct predictor and a facilitating condition.

### 4.2 Social and Contextual Determinants

**Subjective Norms and Social Influence.** The role of subjective norms—perceived social pressure from family, friends, and significant others—shows considerable variability across studies and contexts. While some studies find significant positive effects [5], [6], others report weak or non-significant relationships [16], [17], [30]. Cross-cultural research suggests that subjective norms are less important predictors than attitudes and PBC, with their influence varying by cultural dimensions such as individualism-collectivism [30]. In Colombia, subjective norms showed no direct influence on EI but demonstrated indirect effects through personal attitudes and PBC [17].

**Family Background and Role Models.** Family entrepreneurship background consistently predicts higher entrepreneurial intentions. Students with entrepreneur family members show significantly higher EI (mean = 3.6667) compared to those without (mean = 3.0227, p = 0.018) [13]. In Colombia, 71.8% of students with entrepreneurial intentions had a close family member who was an entrepreneur [11]. Having entrepreneur friends also significantly influences EI [2], [18], suggesting that both familial and peer role models shape entrepreneurial aspirations through social learning and normative influence.

**Socioeconomic Factors.** Socioeconomic status shows complex relationships with EI. In Colombia, entrepreneurial intention increases with social class, influenced by university environment and access to resources [18]. Students from higher socioeconomic strata, attending private universities, and with work experience demonstrate greater EI [18]. However, lack of capital and insufficient institutional support remain major obstacles [2], [28], highlighting the tension between aspiration and resource availability in emerging economies.

**Cultural and National Context.** National and regional culture significantly shapes EI and its determinants. Latin American students generally show positive relationships between skills, family background, and risk propensity to entrepreneurship [16]. However, cultural differences influence which TPB components are most salient; for example, the Ajzen model best explains EI in Chile but only partially in Colombia, where subjective norms are not significant [27]. These findings underscore the importance of cultural adaptation in applying EI models across contexts.

### 4.3 Educational and Institutional Determinants

**Entrepreneurship Education.** The impact of entrepreneurship education on EI shows mixed results. Some studies find significant positive effects [7], [23], while others report non-significant or even negative relationships [30]. A multi-country Latin American study found that entrepreneurship education was independent of entrepreneurial intention (p = 0.056), suggesting that supplementary mechanisms beyond education are essential [19]. The effectiveness of entrepreneurship education appears to depend on pedagogical approach, with practical projects and experiential learning showing stronger effects than purely theoretical instruction [25].

**University Support and Ecosystem.** Perceived university support—including educational support, concept development assistance, and business development resources—significantly influences EI both directly and indirectly through attitudes and PBC [5], [7]. The entrepreneurial university environment mediates relationships between individual traits (risk-taking, proactiveness, innovativeness) and EI [1]. University incubators and the presence of successful university-incubated entrepreneurs serve as motivators for student EI [13], highlighting the importance of visible success models within the institutional context.

**Entrepreneurial Skills and Competence.** Entrepreneurial skills and competencies show strong effects on EI. In a study of agricultural students, entrepreneurial skill had the highest effect (63%) on EI, followed by self-efficacy (44%) and attitude (38%) [16]. Entrepreneurship education influences EI through the mediating effect of entrepreneurial competence [14], suggesting that education must successfully develop actual capabilities—not just knowledge—to impact intentions. Leadership skills, in particular, strongly predict EI through perceived desirability and feasibility [26].

## 5. Entrepreneurial Intention in Latin America and Colombia (2010–2026)

### 5.1 Regional Overview: Latin American Context

Latin America represents a dynamic context for entrepreneurial intention research, characterized by high levels of necessity-driven entrepreneurship, significant youth unemployment, and evolving institutional support for entrepreneurship. Studies spanning nine Latin American countries (Argentina, Brazil, Chile, Colombia, Costa Rica, Ecuador, Mexico, Panama, and Uruguay) reveal both common patterns and important cross-national variations.

**Regional Patterns.** A comprehensive study of 9,012 university students across nine Latin American countries found that university context and cognitive variables explain entrepreneurial intentions, with program learning directly and indirectly influencing EI [30]. However, attending entrepreneurship courses showed a negative influence on EI, suggesting potential issues with course design or delivery that may discourage rather than inspire entrepreneurship [30]. This counterintuitive finding highlights the need for careful evaluation of entrepreneurship education programs in the region.

Latin American students generally demonstrate positive relationships between skills, family background, and risk propensity to entrepreneurship [16]. However, the region faces persistent challenges in translating intentions into actual venture creation, with administrative procedures, lack of business experience, and insufficient financing identified as primary inhibitors [7]. Cultural collectivism in some Latin American countries (e.g., Argentina) fosters well-being and may influence entrepreneurial motivations differently than in more individualistic cultures [8].

**Cross-National Variations.** Significant variations exist across Latin American countries in both the level of EI and the relative importance of different determinants. Venezuelan and Colombian students show higher entrepreneurial intentions compared to students from other Latin American countries [15]. In Chile, the Ajzen model best explains EI, while in Colombia, subjective norms are not significant, suggesting cultural differences in the role of social influence [27]. These variations underscore the importance of country-specific research and the limitations of pan-regional generalizations.

### 5.2 Colombian Studies: Key Findings

Colombia has been the focus of extensive EI research, with studies spanning multiple regions and universities. The country's high entrepreneurial activity rates, combined with persistent challenges in business sustainability, make it a critical context for understanding EI dynamics in emerging economies.

**National-Level Studies.** The most comprehensive Colombian study analyzed 12,383 university students from 57 national universities using GUESSS 2018-2019 data [16]. This study found that Personal Attitudes (PAs) and Perceived Behavioral Control (PBC) are significant determinants of EI, while Subjective Norms (SNs) have no direct influence but show indirect effects [16]. Colombian students exhibited a relatively pessimistic view of EI, with many lacking incentives to pursue entrepreneurship [16]. This pessimism may reflect realistic assessments of the challenges facing entrepreneurs in Colombia, including limited access to capital, bureaucratic obstacles, and market uncertainties.

**Regional Studies: Quindío.** Multiple studies have focused on university students in the Department of Quindío, providing detailed insights into regional dynamics. A study of 297 last-semester students found that entrepreneurial intention is influenced by type of university (private), having an entrepreneur friend, social norms favoring entrepreneurship over employment, and positive images of entrepreneurs [2], [18], [28]. Obstacles include lack of capital, insufficient institutional support, academic obligations, and lack of clarity on business ideas or partners [2], [28]. Older students in private universities with work experience, higher socioeconomic status, married status, and entrepreneurial family members showed greater EI [18].

**Regional Studies: Medellín and Valle del Cauca.** Studies in Medellín and Valle del Cauca reveal the importance of contextual factors. In Valle del Cauca, 70% of university students expressed interest in sustainable entrepreneurship, with family support emerging as the most valued variable [6]. Students in smaller, non-capital cities showed positive social norm perception influencing entrepreneurial intent [6]. In Medellín, student attitudes, current behavioral control, perceived behavioral control, and entrepreneurial behavior significantly influence EI, with personal attitude being the most important factor [9].

**Bogotá Studies.** Research in Bogotá has examined the role of leadership skills and social capital. A study of 322 undergraduate business management students found that leadership skills (bridging cognitive social capital) strongly and significantly predict EI through perceived desirability and feasibility [26]. Interestingly, family entrepreneurship background showed no indirect association with intentions, possibly due to high socio-cultural acceptance of entrepreneurship in Colombia [26]. This suggests that in contexts where entrepreneurship is already highly valued, family background may be less differentiating than in contexts where entrepreneurship is less socially legitimate.

**Key Determinants in Colombian Context.** Across Colombian studies, several determinants consistently emerge as significant:

1. **Personal Attitudes:** The strongest and most consistent predictor of EI in Colombian samples [9], [16], [17].

2. **Perceived Behavioral Control:** Significant positive influence, though effect sizes vary [16], [17].

3. **Family and Social Support:** Family entrepreneurship background and entrepreneur friends significantly influence EI [2], [6], [11], [18].

4. **University Type and Environment:** Private universities and supportive university environments enhance EI [2], [18].

5. **Socioeconomic Factors:** Higher socioeconomic status, work experience, and access to resources positively relate to EI [18].

6. **Country Context:** Colombia's current economic situation and national context significantly influence EI [5].

**Validation of Measurement Instruments.** The Entrepreneurial Intention Questionnaire (EIQ) has been validated in Colombian samples, demonstrating high reliability and predictive validity [11]. This validation confirms that established EI measurement instruments function appropriately in the Colombian context, facilitating cross-cultural comparisons and meta-analytic research.

### 5.3 Cross-National Comparisons within Latin America

Comparative studies within Latin America reveal important insights into how cultural, economic, and institutional differences shape EI across the region.

**Chile-Colombia Comparison.** A comparative study of 351 business management students from Chile and Colombia found that the Ajzen model best explains EI in Chile but only partially in Colombia, where subjective norms are not significant [27]. Social legitimacy of entrepreneurship (SLE) mediates attitude toward entrepreneurship (ATE) for both countries, with Chile showing stronger mediating effects [27]. Gender moderates perceived behavioral control in Chile (a less male-stereotype culture) but not in Colombia [27]. These findings highlight how cultural differences—even within the same region—influence the relative importance of different EI determinants.

**Multi-Country Latin American Studies.** A study of 1,493 undergraduate business students from Chile, Colombia, Ecuador, Peru, and Venezuela found that leadership skills, risk propensity, and internal locus of control significantly predict EI [15]. Contrary to research in other regions, no gender differences in EI were found [15]. Family background, formation level, and country of origin correlated with EI, with Venezuelan and Colombian students showing higher intentions [15]. This suggests that country-level factors—possibly including economic conditions, institutional quality, and cultural norms—significantly shape EI beyond individual-level determinants.

**Implications for Regional Policy.** The cross-national variations within Latin America suggest that entrepreneurship policies and educational interventions cannot be uniformly applied across the region. While some determinants (attitudes, PBC, leadership skills) appear relatively universal, others (subjective norms, gender effects, policy perception) show significant context-dependence. Effective interventions must be tailored to specific national and regional contexts, accounting for cultural values, institutional quality, and economic conditions.

## 6. Contemporary Models and Emerging Factors (2020–2026)

### 6.1 COVID-19 Pandemic Effects

The COVID-19 pandemic has profoundly impacted entrepreneurial ecosystems worldwide, creating both challenges and opportunities that have influenced university students' entrepreneurial intentions. Contemporary research (2020–2026) has begun to examine these effects, revealing complex dynamics.

**Pandemic as a Contextual Factor.** Trif and colleagues examined students' EI during the COVID-19 pandemic and post-pandemic times, finding that the entrepreneurial university environment mediates relationships between individual traits (risk-taking, proactiveness, innovativeness) and EI [1]. The study employed structural equation modeling and Gaussian graphical models on data collected during November 2020–February 2021, capturing the acute phase of the pandemic [1]. The findings suggest that institutional support became even more critical during the crisis, as students faced heightened uncertainty and resource constraints.

**Youth Unemployment and Necessity Entrepreneurship.** The pandemic exacerbated youth unemployment, particularly in emerging economies, intensifying interest in entrepreneurship as an alternative to traditional employment [20]. Studies in Uzbekistan explicitly addressed how the pandemic increased the salience of entrepreneurship education as a policy response to unemployment [20]. However, the shift toward necessity-driven entrepreneurship raises questions about the quality and sustainability of ventures created under crisis conditions.

**Digital Transformation Acceleration.** The pandemic accelerated digital transformation across all sectors, creating new opportunities for digital entrepreneurship while simultaneously requiring new competencies. Contemporary models increasingly incorporate digital literacy, online business models, and virtual collaboration capabilities as relevant factors for EI [4]. The shift to remote learning also transformed how entrepreneurship education is delivered, with implications for its effectiveness in fostering EI.

### 6.2 Sustainability and Social Entrepreneurship

Sustainability has emerged as a critical dimension of contemporary entrepreneurship, with growing recognition that entrepreneurial activity must address environmental and social challenges alongside economic objectives.

**Sustainable Entrepreneurial Intention Models.** Recent studies have extended classic EI models to examine sustainable entrepreneurial intentions specifically. An integrated TPB-EEM model for sustainable entrepreneurship found that attitude, subjective norm, and behavioral control significantly influence perceived desirability and feasibility, which in turn predict sustainable entrepreneurial intention [10]. In Colombia, 70% of university students in Valle del Cauca expressed interest in sustainable entrepreneurship, with family support being the most valued variable [6], [22].

**Social Entrepreneurship Motivations.** Research on social entrepreneurial intentions reveals distinct motivational patterns compared to commercial entrepreneurship. A study of American business students found that perceived desirability (β = 0.32, p = 0.00), perceived feasibility (β = 0.36, p = 0.00), and entrepreneurial experience (β = 0.33, p = 0.07) predict social entrepreneurial intention, with desire for self-realization (β = 0.21, p = 0.00) and autonomy (β = 0.15, p = 0.01) determining perceived desirability [11]. Emotional competence and entrepreneurial self-efficacy also play critical roles in social entrepreneurship contexts [11].

**Integration with Sustainable Development Goals.** Contemporary models increasingly integrate awareness of Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) as a driving force for entrepreneurial intentions [19]. This integration reflects growing recognition that entrepreneurship education must prepare students to address global challenges such as climate change, inequality, and resource scarcity, not merely to create profitable businesses.

### 6.3 Digital Entrepreneurship and Innovation

The digital economy has transformed the nature of entrepreneurship, creating new opportunity spaces while requiring different competencies and resources compared to traditional brick-and-mortar ventures.

**Digital Entrepreneurship Factors.** Contemporary models incorporate digital-specific factors, including digital literacy, online platform familiarity, and virtual networking capabilities. A study developed a digital application specifically for information technology students to enhance entrepreneurial intention, demonstrating methodological innovation in using technology not just as a research tool but as an intervention [4]. This approach recognizes that digital natives may respond differently to traditional entrepreneurship education and support mechanisms.

**Innovation and Entrepreneurial Intention.** Personal innovativeness has emerged as a particularly strong predictor in contemporary models. Studies find that innovativeness not only directly predicts EI but also moderates the attitude-intention relationship [5], [9]. A study of undergraduate students found innovativeness to be an effective predictor of entrepreneurial intention, with causal relationships confirmed through confirmatory analysis and multiple regression [25]. This emphasis on innovation reflects the increasing importance of differentiation and value creation in competitive global markets.

**Technology Commercialization and Academic Entrepreneurship.** Research on academic entrepreneurship has examined how training programs influence faculty and graduate student researchers' intentions to commercialize research [10]. These studies reveal that the impact of training is moderated by professional role, prior experience, and project potential [10], suggesting that interventions must be tailored to the specific contexts and needs of different academic populations.

## 7. Public Entrepreneurship Policy and Entrepreneurial Intention: Research Gaps

### 7.1 Policy-EI Relationship: Current Evidence

The relationship between public entrepreneurship policy and entrepreneurial intention has received increasing attention, yet remains one of the least understood aspects of EI research. Existing evidence presents a complex and sometimes contradictory picture.

**Positive Policy Effects.** Several studies report significant positive relationships between perceived policy support and EI. In China, entrepreneurship policy positively and significantly affects college students' EI, with governmental support encouraging students to start businesses by reducing obstacles and enhancing confidence [29]. Familiarity with social entrepreneurial policy significantly enhances students' EI, having the most substantial impact among identified sources [21]. Government programs and support significantly influence EI (β = 0.171, p = 0.024), reinforcing subjective norms and promoting youth participation [19].

The role of entrepreneurship policy in college students' EI shows significant positive correlation (supporting Hypothesis 1 in multiple studies), with policy positively influencing entrepreneurial practice and entrepreneurial spirit [9]. Entrepreneurial policy perception significantly and positively impacts EI, legitimizing entrepreneurship and reducing environmental uncertainty, while also indirectly influencing intention by increasing confidence in entrepreneurial competencies [15].

**Null and Negative Findings.** However, other studies find weak or non-significant policy effects. A study in Tunisia found that governmental entrepreneurial policies are not significant and do not encourage students' EI, attributed to students having theoretical knowledge but lacking practical experience and not perceiving government policies positively [3]. Research in Saudi Arabia found a non-significant moderating role for perceived governmental support on the entrepreneurship education-EI relationship [18]. In Poland, results did not positively verify a direct influence of public policy on entrepreneurial intentions [22].

**Moderating and Mediating Effects.** Policy effects often operate indirectly through mediating or moderating mechanisms. Governmental support significantly moderates the relationship between entrepreneurial education, awareness, inspiration, and orientation with EI, amplifying their positive effects [26]. Entrepreneurial policy moderates the relationship between entrepreneurship education and EI [24]. Entrepreneurial policy support significantly moderates the mediated relationship between improvisation and EI by entrepreneurial self-efficacy [27].

### 7.2 Identified Research Gaps

The literature reveals several critical gaps in understanding the policy-EI relationship:

**Gap 1: Mechanisms of Policy Influence.** While studies acknowledge that entrepreneurial policies are important, there is limited research on *how* policies affect willingness and intention [11]. Existing research has largely focused on institutional supply and policy tools, with limited attention to mechanisms through which policy perception influences EI via internal psychological pathways [15]. Future research should explore the cognitive and affective processes through which policy awareness translates into entrepreneurial motivation and capability beliefs.

**Gap 2: Policy Design and Implementation.** There is a lack of unified publishing of resources and legal guarantees for policy implementation [11]. Studies note potential limitations like bureaucratic inefficiencies or limited awareness/access to policies [19], but systematic research on policy design features that enhance effectiveness is scarce. Questions remain about optimal policy mixes, delivery mechanisms, and targeting strategies.

**Gap 3: Longitudinal Policy Effects.** The vast majority of policy-EI studies employ cross-sectional designs, which can only identify associations, not causality [26]. Future research should use longitudinal studies to examine how governmental initiatives shape EI over time [26], tracking students from policy exposure through intention formation to actual venture creation. This would address the critical question of whether policy effects are sustained or fade over time.

**Gap 4: Context-Specific Policy Effectiveness.** While knowledge is lacking on how public procedures and policies influence young people's entrepreneurial intentions [22], even less is known about how policy effectiveness varies across contexts. Do policies that work in developed economies translate to emerging markets? How do cultural values, institutional quality, and economic conditions moderate policy effects? These questions remain largely unanswered.

**Gap 5: Policy-Education Interactions.** The relationship between entrepreneurship policy and entrepreneurship education is complex and under-explored. Some studies find that policy moderates education effects [24], while others find education and policy operate independently [19]. Understanding how these two major interventions interact—whether they are substitutes, complements, or operate through distinct pathways—is critical for efficient resource allocation.

**Gap 6: Specific Policy Instruments.** Most studies treat "entrepreneurship policy" as a monolithic construct, but policies encompass diverse instruments including financial support (grants, loans, tax incentives), regulatory reforms (business registration simplification), infrastructure provision (incubators, accelerators), and information/training programs. Research examining the differential effects of specific policy instruments is rare, limiting actionable guidance for policymakers.

### 7.3 Methodological Limitations

Several methodological limitations constrain current understanding of policy-EI relationships:

**Measurement Challenges.** Policy perception is typically measured through self-reported awareness or subjective evaluation of policy support. These measures may not accurately reflect actual policy availability or quality, and are susceptible to social desirability bias and individual differences in information-seeking behavior. More objective measures of policy exposure and utilization are needed.

**Endogeneity Concerns.** Cross-sectional correlations between policy perception and EI may reflect reverse causality—students with higher EI may be more likely to seek out and positively evaluate policy support. Experimental or quasi-experimental designs that manipulate policy exposure would provide stronger causal evidence.

**Limited Variance in Policy Context.** Most studies examine policy effects within a single country or region at a single point in time, limiting variance in policy context. Cross-national comparative studies that leverage natural experiments (e.g., policy reforms, regional variations) would provide more robust evidence of policy effects.

**Inadequate Attention to Implementation.** Studies typically focus on policy existence or perception rather than implementation quality. Yet implementation failures—due to bureaucratic obstacles, insufficient funding, poor targeting, or lack of awareness—may explain null findings in some contexts. Process evaluations examining policy implementation alongside outcome evaluations of EI would provide more complete understanding.

## 8. Discussion and Synthesis

This comprehensive literature mapping reveals both substantial progress and persistent challenges in understanding entrepreneurial intention among university students. Several key themes emerge from the synthesis of 432 studies spanning 2010–2026.

**Theoretical Maturity and Integration.** The field has achieved considerable theoretical maturity, with robust evidence supporting the core propositions of TPB and EEM across diverse contexts. The integration of these foundational theories into unified frameworks represents a significant advance, moving beyond debates about which model is "better" toward recognition that they capture complementary aspects of intention formation. Contemporary extensions incorporating personality traits, educational factors, and contextual variables have enriched these models without abandoning their theoretical core.

**Universal and Context-Specific Determinants.** The evidence reveals both universal and context-specific patterns. Personal attitudes and perceived behavioral control emerge as culture-universal predictors, consistently showing strong effects across countries, cultures, and time periods. However, the role of subjective norms, family background, and policy support varies substantially across contexts, suggesting that these factors are more culturally and institutionally embedded. This pattern has important implications: interventions targeting attitudes and self-efficacy may be broadly applicable, while those leveraging social influence or policy support must be carefully adapted to local contexts.

**The Latin American and Colombian Context.** Latin American and Colombian studies reveal high levels of entrepreneurial intention but persistent challenges in translating intentions into sustainable ventures. The region's high necessity-driven entrepreneurship, combined with resource constraints and institutional weaknesses, creates a distinctive context where aspiration outpaces capability and support. Colombian students demonstrate strong personal attitudes toward entrepreneurship and reasonable levels of perceived behavioral control, yet face significant obstacles including lack of capital, insufficient institutional support, and bureaucratic barriers. This gap between intention and enablement represents a critical policy challenge.

**The Policy Puzzle.** Perhaps the most striking finding is the inconsistent and often weak relationship between public entrepreneurship policy and entrepreneurial intention. While some studies report significant positive effects, others find null or even negative relationships. This inconsistency likely reflects several factors: (1) genuine variation in policy quality and implementation across contexts, (2) measurement challenges in capturing policy exposure and perception, (3) complex mediating and moderating mechanisms that are not fully understood, and (4) the possibility that policy effects operate primarily on the intention-behavior translation rather than on intention formation itself. Resolving this puzzle is critical, given the substantial public resources invested in entrepreneurship policy worldwide.

**The Intention-Behavior Gap.** A persistent challenge across all EI research is the gap between intention and actual entrepreneurial behavior. While intentions are necessary precursors to behavior, they are far from sufficient. Contextual factors, resource availability, opportunity recognition, and implementation planning all influence whether intentions translate into action. Contemporary models that explicitly address this gap through inclusion of situational factors and implementation intentions represent important progress, but much work remains to understand the conditions under which intentions become behaviors.

**Emerging Factors and Future Challenges.** The incorporation of sustainability, digital entrepreneurship, and pandemic effects into contemporary models reflects the field's responsiveness to evolving economic and social realities. However, these extensions also risk theoretical fragmentation if not carefully integrated into coherent frameworks. The challenge moving forward is to maintain theoretical parsimony while acknowledging the increasing complexity of entrepreneurial contexts.

## 9. Future Research Directions

Based on the identified gaps and limitations, several priority directions for future research emerge:

**1. Longitudinal and Experimental Designs.** The field urgently needs more longitudinal studies tracking students from intention formation through venture creation, and experimental or quasi-experimental studies that manipulate key variables (education, policy exposure, mentoring) to establish causal relationships. Such designs would address the fundamental limitation of cross-sectional correlational research and provide stronger evidence for intervention effectiveness.

**2. Policy Mechanism Research.** Future research should move beyond documenting policy-EI correlations to examining the mechanisms through which policies influence intentions and behaviors. This includes: (a) cognitive pathways (how policy awareness shapes opportunity perception and capability beliefs), (b) affective pathways (how policy support influences entrepreneurial identity and emotional commitment), (c) behavioral pathways (how policy resources enable concrete preparatory actions), and (d) social pathways (how policy legitimizes entrepreneurship and shapes social norms).

**3. Comparative Policy Research.** Cross-national comparative studies that leverage natural experiments—policy reforms, regional variations, or temporal changes—would provide robust evidence of policy effects while illuminating contextual moderators. Such research should examine specific policy instruments (financial support, regulatory reform, infrastructure, training) rather than treating policy as monolithic, enabling more actionable guidance for policymakers.

**4. Implementation Science Approaches.** Applying implementation science frameworks to entrepreneurship policy would illuminate why policies succeed or fail in practice. Process evaluations examining policy awareness, accessibility, utilization, and quality alongside outcome evaluations of EI and venture creation would provide more complete understanding and identify leverage points for improvement.

**5. Intention-Behavior Translation.** Research should focus more explicitly on the intention-behavior gap, examining: (a) what proportion of students with high EI actually attempt venture creation, (b) what factors facilitate or impede this translation, (c) how long the process takes, and (d) whether interventions can be designed to bridge the gap. This may require following students beyond graduation into early career stages.

**6. Heterogeneity and Personalization.** Most EI research examines average effects across samples, but substantial heterogeneity likely exists in how different students respond to education, policy, and other interventions. Research examining individual differences (personality, prior experience, discipline, career stage) that moderate intervention effects would enable more personalized and efficient approaches.

**7. Sustainability and Social Entrepreneurship.** As sustainability challenges intensify, research should examine how to foster sustainable and social entrepreneurial intentions specifically, not just general EI. This includes understanding the distinct motivations, capabilities, and support needs of students oriented toward social and environmental value creation.

**8. Digital and Technology Entrepreneurship.** The digital transformation of entrepreneurship requires updated models that account for digital-specific factors, including online platform literacy, virtual networking, and technology commercialization. Research should also examine how digital tools can enhance entrepreneurship education and support delivery.

**9. Emerging Economy Contexts.** While research in emerging economies has increased, developed Western contexts remain overrepresented. More research in Africa, Asia, and Latin America—particularly studies that examine context-specific challenges and opportunities rather than simply replicating Western models—would enhance the field's global relevance and applicability.

**10. Mixed-Methods Integration.** Combining quantitative modeling with qualitative inquiry would provide richer understanding of EI processes. Qualitative research can illuminate the lived experiences, decision-making processes, and contextual nuances that quantitative models may miss, while quantitative research can test the generalizability of qualitative insights.

---

## 10. Conclusion

This comprehensive literature mapping of entrepreneurial intention research among university students reveals a field that has achieved substantial theoretical and empirical maturity while confronting persistent challenges and emerging opportunities. The evolution from foundational theories—Ajzen's Theory of Planned Behavior (1991) and Shapero & Sokol's Entrepreneurial Event Model (1982)—to contemporary integrated models incorporating sustainability, digital transformation, and global disruptions demonstrates the field's theoretical vitality and practical relevance.

Key findings include: (1) personal attitudes and perceived behavioral control are robust, culture-universal predictors of EI, while subjective norms and policy effects show greater context-dependence; (2) Latin American and Colombian students demonstrate high entrepreneurial intentions but face significant obstacles in translating intentions into sustainable ventures; (3) the relationship between public entrepreneurship policy and EI remains inconsistent and poorly understood, representing a critical gap given substantial public investments in entrepreneurship support; and (4) contemporary factors including sustainability, digital entrepreneurship, and pandemic effects are reshaping entrepreneurial contexts and requiring theoretical extensions.

For researchers, this review identifies priority directions including longitudinal and experimental designs, policy mechanism research, implementation science approaches, and greater attention to the intention-behavior gap. For policymakers, the findings suggest that while policy can influence EI, effects are often indirect, context-dependent, and contingent on implementation quality. Effective policy requires not just resource provision but also attention to how policies shape cognitive perceptions, social norms, and institutional environments. For educators, the evidence supports entrepreneurship education that develops actual capabilities and self-efficacy through experiential learning, not just knowledge transmission through lectures.

The Colombian context, with Dataset ALBA 2025 (N=540 university students, Zenodo DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.17702439), exemplifies both the promise and challenges of fostering entrepreneurship in emerging economies. Colombian students possess strong personal attitudes toward entrepreneurship and reasonable perceived behavioral control, yet face resource constraints, institutional weaknesses, and policy implementation gaps that impede venture creation. Addressing these challenges requires coordinated efforts across education, policy, and ecosystem development, informed by rigorous research on what works, for whom, and under what conditions.

As entrepreneurship becomes increasingly central to economic development, innovation, and social problem-solving, understanding and fostering entrepreneurial intention among university students—the next generation of entrepreneurs—takes on heightened importance. This literature mapping provides a comprehensive foundation for researchers, policymakers, and educators seeking to advance both knowledge and practice in this critical domain.
