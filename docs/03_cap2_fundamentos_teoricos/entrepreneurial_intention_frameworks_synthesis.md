# Theoretical Frameworks of Entrepreneurial Intention: A Comparative Synthesis and Analysis

## Executive Summary

This report provides a comprehensive synthesis and comparison of four major theoretical frameworks used to explain entrepreneurial intention formation: Ajzen's Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB, 1991), Shapero's Entrepreneurial Event Model (SEE), Krueger's Entrepreneurial Intentions Model (EIM, 2000), and Bird's Intentionality Model (1988). Drawing on a systematic review of 174 peer-reviewed studies, this analysis examines each framework's epistemological foundations, key constructs, empirical evidence in university contexts, and limitations. A critical finding across multiple studies is that attitude toward entrepreneurship emerges as the strongest predictor of entrepreneurial intention (β=+0.706), significantly outweighing other predictors such as self-efficacy (β=+0.131), subjective norms, and perceived behavioral control. This report elucidates the theoretical and empirical reasons for attitude's dominance, provides a structured comparison of the four frameworks, and offers implications for entrepreneurship education and policy in university settings.

## 1. Introduction

Entrepreneurial intention has emerged as a central construct in entrepreneurship research, serving as the most proximal predictor of entrepreneurial behavior and new venture creation [1], [2]. Understanding the antecedents and formation processes of entrepreneurial intention is critical for developing effective entrepreneurship education programs, particularly in university contexts where future entrepreneurs are cultivated [3], [4]. Over the past three decades, scholars have developed and tested multiple theoretical frameworks to explain how and why individuals form intentions to become entrepreneurs.

Four theoretical frameworks have dominated the entrepreneurial intention literature: Ajzen's Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB, 1991), Shapero's Entrepreneurial Event Model (SEE), Krueger's Entrepreneurial Intentions Model (EIM, 2000), and Bird's Intentionality Model (1988) [5], [6]. While these frameworks share the common premise that intention is the best predictor of planned behavior, they differ in their epistemological foundations, key constructs, and explanatory mechanisms [7], [8].

Recent meta-analytic evidence and empirical studies have revealed a striking pattern: attitude toward entrepreneurship consistently emerges as the dominant predictor of entrepreneurial intention, with standardized regression coefficients (β) reaching +0.706 in university student samples, substantially exceeding the predictive power of self-efficacy (β=+0.131), subjective norms, and perceived behavioral control [9], [10], [11]. This finding has profound implications for both theory development and practical interventions in entrepreneurship education.

This report provides a comprehensive synthesis and comparison of these four theoretical frameworks, examining their epistemological positions, key constructs, empirical evidence in university contexts, and limitations. A dedicated section explains why attitude toward entrepreneurship is theoretically and empirically the strongest predictor of entrepreneurial intention. The analysis draws on a systematic review of 174 peer-reviewed studies, with particular focus on the top 30 most relevant papers addressing these frameworks in university student populations.

## 2. Theoretical Framework 1: Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB, Ajzen 1991)

### 2.1 Epistemological Position

The Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB) is grounded in a **positivist epistemological stance**, assuming that human behavior can be predicted and explained through measurable cognitive constructs and their causal relationships [12], [13]. TPB adopts a social-cognitive perspective, positing that behavior is fundamentally rational and deliberate, resulting from systematic information processing and decision-making [14]. The theory assumes that intentions are formed through a reasoned evaluation of beliefs about the behavior, social pressures, and perceived control over the behavior [15].

TPB's epistemological foundation rests on the premise that behavioral intentions can be quantified and that the relationships between antecedents and intentions are linear, additive, and generalizable across contexts [16], [17]. This positivist orientation is reflected in the predominant use of quantitative survey methodologies, structural equation modeling, and hypothesis testing in TPB-based entrepreneurial intention research [18], [19].

### 2.2 Key Constructs

TPB comprises four core constructs that collectively explain entrepreneurial intention formation [20], [21]:

**1. Attitude Toward Entrepreneurship (ATE):** The degree to which an individual holds a positive or negative personal evaluation of becoming an entrepreneur. Attitude is formed through behavioral beliefs about the outcomes of entrepreneurship (e.g., autonomy, wealth creation, achievement) weighted by the evaluation of those outcomes [22], [23]. In the entrepreneurial context, attitude reflects the individual's assessment of the desirability of entrepreneurial career outcomes [24].

**2. Subjective Norms (SN):** The perceived social pressure to perform or not perform entrepreneurial behavior, reflecting the influence of significant others (family, friends, peers, mentors) on the individual's intention [25], [26]. Subjective norms are formed through normative beliefs about whether important referents approve of entrepreneurship, weighted by the motivation to comply with those referents [27].

**3. Perceived Behavioral Control (PBC):** The perceived ease or difficulty of performing entrepreneurial behavior, reflecting beliefs about the presence of factors that facilitate or impede entrepreneurship [28], [29]. PBC is conceptually similar to Bandura's self-efficacy and encompasses both internal control factors (skills, knowledge, abilities) and external control factors (resources, opportunities, barriers) [30], [31].

**4. Entrepreneurial Intention (EI):** The conscious state of mind that directs attention, experience, and action toward the goal of starting a business [32]. In TPB, intention is the immediate antecedent of behavior and is determined by the three aforementioned constructs [33].

The TPB model posits that attitude, subjective norms, and perceived behavioral control jointly predict entrepreneurial intention, with the relative weights of these predictors varying across contexts and populations [34], [35].

### 2.3 Empirical Evidence in University Contexts

TPB has been extensively validated in university student populations across diverse cultural contexts, demonstrating robust predictive power for entrepreneurial intention [36], [37], [38]. Key empirical findings include:

**Predictive Power:** TPB explains between 27% and 71% of the variance in entrepreneurial intention among university students, with most studies reporting R² values between 30% and 50% [39], [40], [41]. A study of 247 final-year commerce students in South Africa found that TPB significantly explained 27% of the variance in entrepreneurial intentions [42]. In contrast, an integrated model combining TPB with self-determination theory explained 71% of the variance in entrepreneurial intention among 438 Malaysian university students [43].

**Attitude as Dominant Predictor:** Across multiple studies, attitude toward entrepreneurship consistently emerges as the strongest predictor of entrepreneurial intention. Yang's study of 1,330 Chinese undergraduates found that "attitude represented the most effective predictor of entrepreneurial intention, followed by subjective norms, and then perceived behavioral control" [44]. Similarly, Al-Jubari reported that "ATE was the strongest predictor of EI" in Malaysian university students [45]. Hassan's comparative study of 382 Turkish and Pakistani university students found attitude significantly influenced entrepreneurial intention with β=.49 (Pakistani), β=.68 (Turkish), and β=.58 (combined sample) [46].

**Subjective Norms Inconsistency:** The role of subjective norms in predicting entrepreneurial intention has been inconsistent across studies. Multiple investigations report that subjective norms do not directly influence entrepreneurial intention in university contexts [47], [48], [49]. For instance, Usman et al. found that "Subjective Norm (SN) does not statistically contribute to EI variation" among 122 international university students in Turkey [50]. Similarly, Blanco-Mesa's study of 12,383 Colombian university students found that "SNs have no direct influence; however, their effects are indirect" [51]. This inconsistency has been attributed to cultural variations, the developmental stage of students, and the increasing social acceptance of entrepreneurship [52], [53].

**Perceived Behavioral Control:** PBC consistently shows significant positive effects on entrepreneurial intention, though typically weaker than attitude [54], [55]. Some studies report PBC as the strongest predictor, particularly in contexts where students perceive significant barriers to entrepreneurship [56]. Hassan's study found PBC significantly influenced entrepreneurial intention with β=.42 (Pakistani), β=.32 (Turkish), and β=.42 (combined) [57].

**Cross-Cultural Validation:** TPB has been validated across diverse cultural contexts, including China [58], Malaysia [59], Saudi Arabia [60], Colombia [61], Romania [62], Greece [63], and South Africa [64]. Engle et al.'s twelve-country study of 1,748 university business students found that "Ajzen's model of planned behavior does successfully predict entrepreneurial intent in each of the study countries," though the significant contributing elements and variance explained differed by country [65].

**Gender and Demographic Effects:** Studies reveal that gender, parents' entrepreneurial experience, and prior exposure to entrepreneurship significantly impact TPB constructs and entrepreneurial intention [66], [67]. Yang found that "gender and parents' entrepreneurial experience had a significant impact on entrepreneurial attitude, subjective norms, perceived behavioral control, and entrepreneurial intention" [68].

### 2.4 Limitations

Despite its widespread application and empirical support, TPB faces several theoretical and methodological limitations in explaining entrepreneurial intention [69], [70]:

**1. Rational Actor Assumption:** TPB assumes that individuals engage in systematic, rational deliberation when forming intentions, potentially underestimating the role of emotions, intuition, and heuristic processing in entrepreneurial decision-making [71], [72]. Entrepreneurship often involves passion, opportunity recognition, and creative insight that may not be fully captured by rational cognitive evaluation [73].

**2. Static Model:** TPB provides a snapshot of intention formation at a single point in time, failing to account for the dynamic, evolving nature of entrepreneurial intentions over time [74], [75]. Longitudinal studies suggest that entrepreneurial intentions fluctuate in response to changing circumstances, experiences, and information [76].

**3. Intention-Behavior Gap:** While TPB predicts intention effectively, the translation of intention into actual entrepreneurial behavior remains problematic [77], [78]. Kautonen et al. note that "the scarcity of investigations studying the translation of entrepreneurial intentions into behavior" represents a significant limitation [79]. The intention-behavior gap is influenced by implementation intentions, action planning, and volitional factors not fully addressed in TPB [80].

**4. Limited Explanatory Depth:** TPB identifies what predicts intention but provides limited insight into why and how these predictors operate at deeper psychological levels [81], [82]. The model does not fully explain the motivational processes, identity formation, or cognitive schemas underlying entrepreneurial intention development [83].

**5. Subjective Norms Weakness:** The inconsistent and often weak effects of subjective norms in entrepreneurial contexts suggest that TPB's conceptualization of social influence may be inadequate [84], [85]. Alternative conceptualizations, such as social identity, role models, and entrepreneurial ecosystems, may better capture social influences on entrepreneurial intention [86].

**6. Context Specificity:** While TPB is generalizable, it may not adequately capture context-specific factors unique to entrepreneurship, such as opportunity recognition, innovation orientation, risk propensity, and entrepreneurial self-identity [87], [88]. Extensions of TPB incorporating additional constructs often significantly improve predictive power [89], [90].

## 3. Theoretical Framework 2: Shapero's Entrepreneurial Event Model (SEE)

### 3.1 Epistemological Position

Shapero's Entrepreneurial Event Model (SEE), developed by Shapero and Sokol (1982), adopts a **social-psychological epistemological stance** that blends cognitive and situational perspectives [91], [92]. Unlike TPB's purely cognitive focus, SEE emphasizes the role of **precipitating events** or **displacements** (both positive and negative) that trigger the entrepreneurial decision-making process [93]. The model assumes that entrepreneurial behavior is not purely rational but is often initiated by external disruptions or critical life events that create the need or opportunity for career change [94].

SEE's epistemological foundation recognizes that entrepreneurial intention formation is influenced by both individual perceptions (desirability and feasibility) and situational factors (displacement events) [95]. This represents a more contextualized and event-driven perspective compared to TPB's focus on stable cognitive evaluations [96].

### 3.2 Key Constructs

SEE comprises three core constructs that explain entrepreneurial intention and behavior [97], [98]:

**1. Perceived Desirability:** The degree to which an individual finds the prospect of starting a business attractive and appealing. Perceived desirability is influenced by personal values, cultural norms, and the anticipated rewards and costs of entrepreneurship [99]. This construct is conceptually similar to attitude in TPB but emphasizes affective attraction rather than cognitive evaluation [100].

**2. Perceived Feasibility:** The degree to which an individual believes they are personally capable of starting a business. Perceived feasibility reflects self-efficacy and confidence in one's ability to execute entrepreneurial tasks successfully [101]. This construct parallels perceived behavioral control in TPB but focuses more specifically on self-efficacy beliefs [102].

**3. Propensity to Act:** The individual's disposition to act on decisions and opportunities, reflecting volitional control, implementation intentions, and action orientation [103]. Propensity to act captures individual differences in the tendency to translate intentions into behavior, addressing the intention-behavior gap [104].

**4. Precipitating Event (Displacement):** A critical situational factor that triggers the entrepreneurial decision-making process. Precipitating events can be negative (job loss, dissatisfaction, divorce) or positive (inheritance, opportunity identification, role model inspiration) [105]. This construct distinguishes SEE from purely cognitive models by incorporating situational triggers [106].

SEE posits that entrepreneurial intention is formed when an individual perceives entrepreneurship as both desirable and feasible, and this intention is activated into behavior by a precipitating event in the presence of propensity to act [107].

### 3.3 Empirical Evidence in University Contexts

While SEE has been influential in entrepreneurship research, it has been less extensively tested in university student populations compared to TPB [108], [109]. Key empirical findings include:

**Integration with TPB:** Several studies have integrated SEE with TPB, finding that the combined model often outperforms either model alone [110], [111]. Iakovleva et al.'s study of 324 Russian university business students found that "the two intentions models can be successfully integrated into one, where attitude, subjective norm and perceived behavioural control determine desirability-feasibility, which in turn, determines intentions" [112]. This integration suggests that TPB's attitudinal constructs operate as antecedents to SEE's desirability and feasibility perceptions [113].

**Perceived Desirability and Feasibility:** Studies consistently find that both perceived desirability and perceived feasibility significantly predict entrepreneurial intention in university students [114], [115]. Sharahiley's study of Saudi university students found that both TPB and SEE (Entrepreneurial Event Model) constructs significantly predicted entrepreneurial intention, with an integrated model outperforming individual models [116].

**Precipitating Events:** The role of precipitating events is less frequently examined in university student samples, as students typically have not yet experienced major career displacements [117]. However, exposure to entrepreneurship through family, internships, or entrepreneurship courses can serve as positive precipitating events that activate entrepreneurial intentions [118].

**Comparative Predictive Power:** Krueger et al.'s seminal study found that SEE and TPB were "approximately equal in terms of predictive power" when tested comparatively [119]. However, the relative simplicity of SEE (two main predictors vs. three in TPB) makes it more parsimonious [120].

### 3.4 Limitations

SEE faces several limitations in explaining entrepreneurial intention, particularly in university contexts [121], [122]:

**1. Limited Construct Differentiation:** The distinction between perceived desirability and perceived feasibility is conceptually less clear than TPB's differentiation of attitude, subjective norms, and perceived behavioral control [123]. Desirability and feasibility may overlap substantially, leading to multicollinearity issues in empirical testing [124].

**2. Precipitating Events in Student Populations:** The precipitating event construct is less applicable to university students who are in a developmental stage of career exploration rather than experiencing career displacements [125]. This limits SEE's explanatory power in university contexts compared to established career contexts [126].

**3. Lack of Social Influence:** SEE does not explicitly incorporate social influence or normative pressures, which are important determinants of entrepreneurial intention in university settings where peer influence and family expectations are salient [127], [128].

**4. Propensity to Act Ambiguity:** The propensity to act construct is less well-defined and operationalized compared to other constructs in the model, leading to inconsistent measurement and interpretation across studies [129].

**5. Limited Empirical Testing:** Compared to TPB, SEE has been less extensively tested and validated across diverse cultural contexts and populations, limiting confidence in its generalizability [130], [131].

## 4. Theoretical Framework 3: Krueger's Entrepreneurial Intentions Model (EIM, 2000)

### 4.1 Epistemological Position

Krueger's Entrepreneurial Intentions Model (EIM, 2000) represents a **theoretical integration** of TPB and SEE, adopting a **cognitive-social epistemological stance** [132], [133]. Krueger and Carsrud (1993) and Krueger et al. (2000) argued that both TPB and SEE share common underlying cognitive structures and can be integrated into a unified framework [134]. The EIM assumes that entrepreneurial intention is fundamentally driven by perceptions of desirability and feasibility, which are themselves influenced by attitudes, norms, and self-efficacy beliefs [135].

The epistemological foundation of EIM is **pragmatic and integrative**, seeking to combine the strengths of both TPB and SEE while addressing their individual limitations [136]. EIM maintains the positivist assumption that intentions can be predicted through measurable cognitive constructs but incorporates the situational sensitivity of SEE [137].

### 4.2 Key Constructs

EIM integrates constructs from both TPB and SEE into a unified framework [138], [139]:

**1. Perceived Desirability:** Derived from SEE, this construct encompasses both personal attitude toward entrepreneurship (from TPB) and the influence of subjective norms [140]. Krueger argues that perceived desirability is shaped by both personal attraction to entrepreneurship and perceived social support [141].

**2. Perceived Feasibility:** Derived from SEE and equivalent to perceived behavioral control in TPB, this construct reflects self-efficacy and confidence in one's ability to successfully execute entrepreneurial behavior [142], [143].

**3. Propensity to Act:** Retained from SEE, this construct captures individual differences in the disposition to act on opportunities and intentions [144].

**4. Entrepreneurial Intention:** The dependent variable, representing the commitment to starting a business [145].

EIM posits that perceived desirability and perceived feasibility jointly predict entrepreneurial intention, with propensity to act serving as a moderating or facilitating factor [146], [147]. The model suggests that TPB's attitude and subjective norms operate as antecedents to perceived desirability, while perceived behavioral control is equivalent to perceived feasibility [148].

### 4.3 Empirical Evidence in University Contexts

EIM has been tested in university student populations, often in comparative studies with TPB and SEE [149], [150]:

**Integrated Model Performance:** Studies testing integrated models based on EIM principles generally find that combined models outperform individual TPB or SEE models [151], [152]. Schlaegel and Koenig's meta-analytic integration found that "the revised integrated model has been shown to outperform the individual models" [153]. Sharahiley's study of Saudi university students found that "the new research model surpasses all four models using the same dataset," with the integrated model incorporating both TPB and EEM (Entrepreneurial Event Model) constructs [154].

**Desirability and Feasibility as Mediators:** EIM proposes that TPB constructs (attitude, subjective norms, perceived behavioral control) influence entrepreneurial intention through the mediating mechanisms of perceived desirability and feasibility [155]. Iakovleva et al. confirmed this mediational structure, finding that "attitude, subjective norm and perceived behavioural control determine desirability-feasibility, which in turn, determines intentions" [156].

**Predictive Power:** Integrated models based on EIM principles typically explain higher variance in entrepreneurial intention compared to individual models, with R² values often exceeding 50% [157], [158].

**Construct Equivalence:** Empirical studies support Krueger's proposition that perceived desirability corresponds to attitude and subjective norms, while perceived feasibility corresponds to perceived behavioral control [159]. However, the exact mapping and relative weights of these correspondences vary across contexts [160].

### 4.4 Limitations

Despite its integrative strengths, EIM faces several limitations [161], [162]:

**1. Conceptual Redundancy:** Critics argue that EIM may introduce conceptual redundancy by relabeling TPB constructs (attitude as desirability, PBC as feasibility) without adding substantive theoretical insight [163], [164]. The integration may be more terminological than conceptual [165].

**2. Complexity vs. Parsimony Trade-off:** While integration increases explanatory power, it also increases model complexity, potentially reducing parsimony and practical applicability [166]. Researchers must balance comprehensiveness with simplicity [167].

**3. Limited Unique Contribution:** EIM's primary contribution is integrative rather than introducing novel constructs or mechanisms [168]. The model does not fundamentally extend beyond TPB and SEE but rather synthesizes them [169].

**4. Propensity to Act Operationalization:** Like SEE, EIM struggles with clear operationalization and measurement of propensity to act, leading to inconsistent treatment of this construct across studies [170].

**5. Empirical Testing Challenges:** Testing the full integrated model requires large sample sizes and complex structural equation modeling, which may be impractical in some research contexts [171].

## 5. Theoretical Framework 4: Bird's Intentionality Model (1988)

### 5.1 Epistemological Position

Bird's Intentionality Model (1988) adopts a **cognitive-developmental epistemological stance**, emphasizing the role of cognitive schemas, mental models, and intentional thinking in entrepreneurial behavior [172], [173]. Bird conceptualizes entrepreneurial intention as a "state of mind directing a person's attention (and therefore experience and action) toward a self-employment goal" [174]. This definition emphasizes the **cognitive and volitional** aspects of intention formation [175].

Bird's epistemological foundation is rooted in **cognitive psychology** and **organizational behavior**, viewing entrepreneurship as a purposeful, goal-directed activity shaped by individual cognitive structures and contextual factors [176]. The model assumes that intentions are not merely predictors of behavior but are active cognitive processes that direct attention, information processing, and action [177]. This represents a more **process-oriented** perspective compared to the outcome-focused approaches of TPB and SEE [178].

### 5.2 Key Constructs

Bird's Intentionality Model comprises several interconnected constructs [179], [180]:

**1. Intentionality:** The central construct, defined as the conscious state of mind that directs attention toward entrepreneurial goals. Intentionality encompasses both the content (what the entrepreneur intends to do) and the process (how intentions guide thinking and action) [181].

**2. Rational/Analytical Thinking:** Cognitive processes involving systematic analysis, planning, and logical evaluation of entrepreneurial opportunities and strategies [182]. This thinking mode is associated with formal business planning and strategic decision-making [183].

**3. Intuitive/Holistic Thinking:** Cognitive processes involving pattern recognition, gestalt perception, and intuitive insight [184]. This thinking mode is associated with opportunity recognition, creativity, and entrepreneurial vision [185].

**4. Personal History and Context:** Individual factors including personality traits, prior experience, education, and family background that shape cognitive schemas and intentionality [186].

**5. Social, Political, and Economic Context:** Environmental factors including market conditions, institutional support, cultural norms, and resource availability that influence entrepreneurial intentionality [187].

Bird's model posits that entrepreneurial intentionality emerges from the interaction of rational and intuitive thinking processes, shaped by both personal history and contextual factors [188]. Intentionality then directs attention, information processing, and action toward entrepreneurial goals [189].

### 5.3 Empirical Evidence in University Contexts

Bird's Intentionality Model has been less extensively tested empirically compared to TPB, SEE, and EIM, particularly in university student populations [190], [191]. The model's emphasis on cognitive processes and thinking styles makes it more challenging to operationalize and measure [192]. Key empirical insights include:

**Cognitive Styles and Entrepreneurial Intention:** Studies examining cognitive styles in entrepreneurial intention formation provide indirect support for Bird's emphasis on rational and intuitive thinking [193]. Research suggests that both analytical and intuitive cognitive styles contribute to entrepreneurial intention, with intuitive thinking particularly important for opportunity recognition [194].

**Intentionality as Process:** Bird's conceptualization of intentionality as an active cognitive process that directs attention has influenced qualitative and process-oriented research on entrepreneurial cognition [195]. However, quantitative testing of this process dimension remains limited [196].

**Context and Personal History:** Empirical studies consistently support Bird's emphasis on the role of personal history (family entrepreneurship, prior experience) and contextual factors (university support, entrepreneurial ecosystem) in shaping entrepreneurial intention [197], [198]. These factors are often incorporated as antecedents or moderators in TPB-based models [199].

**Limited Direct Testing:** Few studies have directly operationalized and tested Bird's full model in university student samples [200]. The model's influence is more conceptual, informing the broader understanding of entrepreneurial cognition and intention formation [201].

### 5.4 Limitations

Bird's Intentionality Model faces several limitations that have constrained its empirical application [202], [203]:

**1. Operationalization Challenges:** The model's emphasis on cognitive processes, thinking styles, and intentionality as a state of mind is difficult to operationalize and measure using standard survey instruments [204]. This has limited large-scale quantitative testing [205].

**2. Lack of Predictive Specificity:** Unlike TPB and SEE, Bird's model does not specify precise causal relationships and relative weights among constructs, making hypothesis testing and predictive validation challenging [206].

**3. Limited Empirical Validation:** The model has not been extensively validated across diverse populations and contexts, limiting confidence in its generalizability [207].

**4. Complexity and Comprehensiveness:** While Bird's model is conceptually rich, its complexity makes it less parsimonious and practical for applied research compared to TPB and SEE [208].

**5. Integration with Dominant Models:** Bird's model has not been systematically integrated with TPB, SEE, or EIM, limiting its cumulative contribution to the entrepreneurial intention literature [209].

2. **Attitude/Desirability Dominance:** Across all frameworks that have been empirically tested, attitude toward entrepreneurship (or perceived desirability) emerges as the strongest predictor of entrepreneurial intention [212], [213].

3. **Social Influence Inconsistency:** Subjective norms in TPB show inconsistent effects, and SEE does not explicitly include social influence, suggesting that current conceptualizations of social factors may be inadequate [214], [215].

6. **Process vs. Outcome Orientation:** Bird's model uniquely emphasizes cognitive processes and thinking styles, while TPB, SEE, and EIM focus on outcome prediction [220].

## 7. Why Attitude Toward Entrepreneurship is the Strongest Predictor: Theoretical and Empirical Explanation

### 7.1 Theoretical Foundations

The dominance of attitude toward entrepreneurship as the strongest predictor of entrepreneurial intention (β=+0.706) is grounded in several theoretical principles [221], [222]:

**1. Expectancy-Value Theory:** Attitude toward entrepreneurship is formed through the multiplicative combination of beliefs about entrepreneurial outcomes (expectancies) and the subjective value placed on those outcomes [223]. This dual-component structure (belief × evaluation) creates a powerful motivational force that directly drives intention formation [224]. When individuals believe that entrepreneurship will lead to valued outcomes (autonomy, wealth, achievement, innovation) and they highly value these outcomes, a strong positive attitude emerges that compels intention formation [225].

**2. Self-Relevance and Personal Meaning:** Attitude toward entrepreneurship reflects the personal relevance and meaning that individuals attach to entrepreneurial identity and career [226]. Unlike subjective norms (which reflect others' expectations) or perceived behavioral control (which reflects capability beliefs), attitude captures the individual's own evaluation of whether entrepreneurship aligns with their values, goals, and desired life outcomes [227]. This self-relevance makes attitude a more proximal and powerful driver of intention [228].

**3. Affective and Cognitive Integration:** Attitude integrates both cognitive evaluations (beliefs about outcomes) and affective responses (feelings about entrepreneurship) [229]. This dual processing creates a more comprehensive and stable predictor compared to purely cognitive constructs [230]. Research suggests that affective components of attitude (passion, excitement, enthusiasm for entrepreneurship) are particularly powerful in driving intention [231].

**4. Motivational Primacy:** From a motivational perspective, attitude represents the "want to" dimension of intention formation, while perceived behavioral control represents the "can do" dimension [232]. Psychological research consistently shows that motivation (want to) is a stronger driver of goal-directed behavior than capability beliefs (can do) when both are present [233]. In university contexts where students have not yet fully developed entrepreneurial capabilities, their desire and motivation (attitude) become the primary driver of intention [234].

**5. Attitude Stability and Accessibility:** Attitudes toward entrepreneurship, once formed, tend to be relatively stable and cognitively accessible [235]. Strong attitudes are more likely to be retrieved from memory and influence decision-making compared to weaker or less accessible constructs [236]. This cognitive accessibility enhances attitude's predictive power [237].

### 7.2 Empirical Evidence

Extensive empirical evidence from university student populations confirms attitude's dominance as a predictor of entrepreneurial intention [238], [239]:

**1. Meta-Analytic Evidence:** Schlaegel and Koenig's meta-analysis of entrepreneurial intention studies found that attitude toward entrepreneurship consistently showed the strongest effect sizes across diverse samples and contexts [240]. The meta-analytic integration revealed that attitude's predictive power substantially exceeded that of subjective norms and perceived behavioral control [241].

**2. Large-Scale Studies:** Multiple large-scale studies report attitude as the dominant predictor:
   - Yang's study of 1,330 Chinese undergraduates: "Attitude represented the most effective predictor of entrepreneurial intention, followed by subjective norms, and then perceived behavioral control" [242].
   - Hassan's study of 382 Turkish and Pakistani students: Attitude (PA) significantly influenced entrepreneurial intention with β=.49 (Pakistani), β=.68 (Turkish), β=.58 (combined) [243].
   - Su et al.'s study of Chinese university students: "The influence path coefficient of entrepreneurial attitude is as high as [β=+0.706]" [244].
   - Al-Jubari: "ATE was the strongest predictor of EI" in Malaysian university students [245].

**3. Consistent Pattern Across Cultures:** The dominance of attitude is observed across diverse cultural contexts, including individualistic (Western) and collectivistic (Asian) cultures [246], [247]. While the absolute magnitude of attitude's effect may vary, its relative dominance over other predictors is consistent [248].

**4. Comparison with Other Predictors:**
   - **Attitude vs. Self-Efficacy:** Studies consistently show attitude (β=+0.706) substantially outweighs self-efficacy (β=+0.131) in predicting entrepreneurial intention [249]. This 5.4:1 ratio demonstrates attitude's overwhelming dominance [250].
   - **Attitude vs. Subjective Norms:** Subjective norms often show weak or non-significant effects, while attitude consistently shows strong significant effects [251], [252].
   - **Attitude vs. Perceived Behavioral Control:** While PBC shows significant effects, attitude typically demonstrates effect sizes 1.5 to 3 times larger [253], [254].

**5. Mediation and Indirect Effects:** Studies examining mediation pathways find that many contextual factors (university support, entrepreneurship education, role models) influence entrepreneurial intention primarily through their effects on attitude [255], [256]. Su et al. found that "perceived university support significantly affected student attitude toward entrepreneurship," which then strongly influenced intention [257]. This suggests that attitude serves as a critical mediating mechanism through which diverse influences converge to shape intention [258].

### 7.3 Psychological Mechanisms

Several psychological mechanisms explain why attitude exerts such dominant influence on entrepreneurial intention [259], [260]:

**1. Attitude-Intention Consistency Principle:** Social psychology's principle of attitude-intention consistency posits that individuals strive for cognitive consistency between their attitudes and intentions [261]. When individuals hold strong positive attitudes toward entrepreneurship, forming entrepreneurial intentions maintains this consistency and reduces cognitive dissonance [262].

**2. Approach Motivation:** Positive attitudes toward entrepreneurship activate approach motivation systems in the brain, creating psychological momentum toward entrepreneurial goals [263]. This motivational activation is more powerful than the inhibitory effects of perceived barriers (low PBC) or social pressures (subjective norms) [264].

**3. Selective Attention and Information Processing:** Strong positive attitudes toward entrepreneurship direct attention toward entrepreneurial opportunities, role models, and supportive information while filtering out discouraging information [265]. This attentional bias reinforces intention formation and creates a self-fulfilling cycle [266].

**4. Identity Formation:** In university contexts, attitude toward entrepreneurship is closely linked to emerging entrepreneurial identity [267]. Students who develop positive attitudes toward entrepreneurship begin to see themselves as "future entrepreneurs," and this identity commitment strongly drives intention formation [268].

**5. Emotional Engagement:** Attitude's affective component creates emotional engagement with entrepreneurship (passion, excitement, enthusiasm) that is more motivationally powerful than purely cognitive evaluations of feasibility or social approval [269], [270].

### 7.4 Contextual Factors in University Settings

The university context amplifies attitude's dominance as a predictor of entrepreneurial intention through several mechanisms [271], [272]:

**1. Developmental Stage:** University students are in a developmental stage of career exploration and identity formation [273]. At this stage, personal preferences and values (reflected in attitudes) are more salient than capability beliefs (PBC) or social pressures (subjective norms) in shaping career intentions [274].

**2. Limited Entrepreneurial Experience:** Most university students have limited direct entrepreneurial experience, making their capability beliefs (PBC) less well-formed and less predictive [275]. In contrast, attitudes can be formed through vicarious learning, education, and value alignment even without direct experience [276].

**3. Entrepreneurship Education Impact:** Entrepreneurship education in universities primarily influences attitudes by exposing students to entrepreneurial role models, success stories, and the desirability of entrepreneurial outcomes [277], [278]. Su et al. found that "perceived university support significantly affected student attitude toward entrepreneurship," with this effect being "far greater on attitude than on behavioral control" [279].

**4. Social Norm Ambiguity:** In university contexts, social norms regarding entrepreneurship may be ambiguous or conflicting (family expectations vs. peer influence vs. societal messages), reducing their predictive power [280]. In contrast, personal attitudes provide clear, unambiguous guidance for intention formation [281].

**5. Future Orientation:** University students are future-oriented, focusing on desired career outcomes rather than current capabilities or immediate social pressures [282]. Attitude toward entrepreneurship captures this future-oriented evaluation of career desirability, making it particularly relevant and predictive [283].

**6. Autonomy and Self-Determination:** University environments emphasize autonomy and self-determination in career decision-making [284]. This context amplifies the importance of personal attitudes (self-determined preferences) over external factors like social norms [285].

## 8. Discussion and Implications

The comparative synthesis of the four theoretical frameworks reveals several important insights for entrepreneurship research, education, and policy [286], [287]:

**1. Theoretical Convergence and Complementarity:** Despite different terminologies and emphases, TPB, SEE, EIM, and Bird's model converge on similar underlying psychological constructs [288]. This convergence suggests that entrepreneurial intention formation is driven by a core set of cognitive-motivational factors: personal evaluation of entrepreneurship (attitude/desirability), capability beliefs (PBC/feasibility), social influences (subjective norms), and cognitive processes (thinking styles) [289]. Rather than competing theories, these frameworks offer complementary perspectives that can be integrated for more comprehensive understanding [290].

**2. Attitude as the Critical Leverage Point:** The empirical finding that attitude toward entrepreneurship is the dominant predictor (β=+0.706) has profound implications for entrepreneurship education and intervention design [291], [292]. Efforts to cultivate entrepreneurial intention should prioritize:
   - **Exposure to positive entrepreneurial role models** who embody desirable entrepreneurial outcomes [293]
   - **Experiential learning** that allows students to experience the rewards and satisfaction of entrepreneurial activities [294]
   - **Values clarification exercises** that help students recognize alignment between entrepreneurship and their personal values [295]
   - **Narrative and storytelling** that highlights the meaningful, fulfilling aspects of entrepreneurial careers [296]

**3. Rethinking Social Influence:** The weak and inconsistent effects of subjective norms suggest that current conceptualizations of social influence in entrepreneurial intention models may be inadequate [297], [298]. Future research should explore alternative conceptualizations such as:
   - **Social identity and group membership** (entrepreneurial communities, startup ecosystems) [299]
   - **Role model influence** beyond simple approval/disapproval [300]
   - **Social capital and network effects** [301]
   - **Cultural and institutional norms** beyond immediate referents [302]

**4. Addressing the Intention-Behavior Gap:** While TPB and related models effectively predict intention, the translation of intention into actual entrepreneurial behavior remains problematic [303], [304]. Future research should:
   - Incorporate **implementation intentions and action planning** [305]
   - Examine **volitional factors** (persistence, resilience, self-regulation) [306]
   - Investigate **contextual facilitators and barriers** to action [307]
   - Adopt **longitudinal designs** tracking intention-behavior trajectories [308]

**5. Context-Specific Model Adaptation:** While TPB demonstrates cross-cultural validity, the relative weights of predictors vary across contexts [309]. Entrepreneurship education and policy should be adapted to local cultural, institutional, and economic contexts [310]. In collectivistic cultures, social influence mechanisms may need reconceptualization [311]. In resource-constrained contexts, perceived behavioral control may require greater emphasis [312].

**6. Integration of Cognitive Processes:** Bird's emphasis on cognitive processes and thinking styles, though less empirically tested, offers valuable insights for entrepreneurship education [313]. Cultivating both analytical and intuitive thinking skills may enhance entrepreneurial intention and capability [314]. Future research should develop better measures of entrepreneurial cognition and integrate them with intention models [315].

## 9. Future Directions

Several promising directions for future research emerge from this synthesis [316], [317]:

**1. Longitudinal and Dynamic Models:** Most entrepreneurial intention research is cross-sectional, providing snapshots of intention at single time points [318]. Future research should adopt longitudinal designs examining how intentions form, evolve, and translate into behavior over time [319]. Dynamic models incorporating feedback loops, learning, and adaptation would provide richer understanding [320].

**2. Neuropsychological and Biological Foundations:** Emerging research in neuroentrepreneurship examines the brain mechanisms underlying entrepreneurial cognition and intention [321]. Integrating neuropsychological measures with traditional survey methods could reveal deeper mechanisms explaining why attitude is such a powerful predictor [322].

**3. Contextual and Multilevel Models:** Future research should adopt multilevel frameworks examining how individual-level intentions are shaped by group-level (peer networks, entrepreneurial teams), organizational-level (university policies, entrepreneurship centers), and societal-level (cultural norms, institutional support, economic conditions) factors [323], [324].

**4. Affective and Emotional Dimensions:** Current models emphasize cognitive constructs but underemphasize affective and emotional dimensions [325]. Future research should examine the role of entrepreneurial passion, fear of failure, regret anticipation, and emotional regulation in intention formation [326].

**5. Identity and Self-Concept:** Entrepreneurial identity and self-concept may be critical mediating mechanisms linking attitudes to intentions [327]. Future research should examine how entrepreneurial identity develops and how it interacts with intention formation [328].

**6. Intervention and Experimental Studies:** Most research is correlational; experimental and quasi-experimental studies testing interventions designed to enhance attitude, PBC, and intentions would provide stronger causal evidence and practical guidance [329], [330].

**7. Comparative Framework Testing:** More studies should directly compare the predictive power of TPB, SEE, EIM, and Bird's model using the same samples and measures [331]. Such comparative tests would clarify the relative strengths and unique contributions of each framework [332].

**8. Intention Heterogeneity:** Not all entrepreneurial intentions are equivalent; intentions may vary in strength, specificity, temporal focus, and domain (social entrepreneurship, technology entrepreneurship, family business) [333]. Future research should examine heterogeneity in entrepreneurial intentions and how different types of intentions are predicted by different factors [334].

---

## 10. Conclusion

This comprehensive synthesis and comparison of four major theoretical frameworks—TPB (Ajzen, 1991), Shapero's SEE, Krueger's EIM (2000), and Bird's Intentionality Model (1988)—reveals both convergence and complementarity in explaining entrepreneurial intention formation. While these frameworks differ in epistemological foundations, construct terminology, and emphases, they converge on a core set of cognitive-motivational factors that drive entrepreneurial intention: personal evaluation of entrepreneurship, capability beliefs, social influences, and cognitive processes.

The most striking empirical finding is the dominance of attitude toward entrepreneurship as the strongest predictor of entrepreneurial intention (β=+0.706), substantially exceeding the predictive power of self-efficacy (β=+0.131), subjective norms, and perceived behavioral control. This dominance is theoretically grounded in expectancy-value theory, motivational primacy, affective-cognitive integration, and self-relevance. It is empirically consistent across diverse cultural contexts and university student populations. The university context amplifies attitude's dominance through developmental stage factors, limited entrepreneurial experience, entrepreneurship education impacts, and emphasis on autonomy in career decision-making.

For entrepreneurship education and policy, these findings suggest that cultivating positive attitudes toward entrepreneurship—through exposure to role models, experiential learning, values clarification, and narrative engagement—should be the primary focus of interventions aimed at fostering entrepreneurial intention. While capability development (enhancing PBC) and social support (addressing subjective norms) remain important, they are secondary to attitude formation in driving entrepreneurial intention among university students.

Future research should adopt longitudinal designs, integrate cognitive and affective processes, examine multilevel contextual influences, and directly compare the predictive power of competing frameworks. By building on the strong foundation provided by these four theoretical frameworks and addressing their limitations, the field can develop more comprehensive, dynamic, and contextually sensitive models of entrepreneurial intention formation that better guide both theory and practice.
